With 1,304 km of track, a R$ 29.4 billion investment, and a 99-year concession, the Paraná project combines heavy engineering, cost reduction, and eligibility for green bonds.

An estimated 38 million tons are expected to move through the Western Export Corridor in its first year of full operation. This volume repositions Paraná on the global map of heavy freight infrastructure. The figure, drawn from demand studies for the Nova Ferroeste project, translates into concrete scale the ambition of connecting Brazil’s central-west to the Port of Paranaguá with broad gauge, diesel-electric locomotives, and trainsets worthy of a heavy-haul railway.

The 1,304-kilometer route will link Maracaju, in Mato Grosso do Sul, to the coast of Paraná, including a strategic branch between Cascavel and Foz do Iguaçu. The project, budgeted at R$ 29.4 billion, will be granted a 99-year concession after an auction on the B3 exchange, transferring to the winning consortium responsibility for revitalizing the existing Ferroeste stretch and building the new segments to Chapecó and Paranaguá.

The scale of the investment is backed by a global context of moderate yet steady growth in the freight rail sector. <a href="https://www.factmr.com/report/freight-rail-infrastructure-market" target="_blank">According to an analysis by consultancy Fact.MR</a>, the global freight rail infrastructure market is set to jump from US$ 43.6 billion in 2026 to US$ 64.5 billion in 2036, at a compound annual rate of 4.0%, sustained by grain and ore corridors in emerging economies and decarbonization mandates in Europe.

Brazil appears in that report with a projected growth of 4.8% per year, anchored precisely by projects such as the expansion of the Carajás Railroad and the consolidation of the North-South corridor. Nova Ferroeste fits into this logic as a piece of a logistics chessboard that seeks to reduce export costs on the Western axis by 28%, relieving highways and transferring tons of soybeans, corn, and containers to hopper cars that glide over higher-capacity tracks.

The coordinator of Paraná’s state railway plan, Luiz Henrique Fagundes, detailed that each 100-car train replaces 357 trucks on the roads, with a direct impact on greenhouse gas emissions. In an interview with the Modal Connection portal, Fagundes also confirmed that the project is eligible for the issuance of Green Bonds, <a href="https://modalconnection.com.br/artigos/o-projeto-nova-ferroeste-e-atualizacao-dos-ramais-ferroviarios/" target="_blank">Green Bonds</a>, a financial instrument that links the cost of capital to meeting verifiable environmental targets.

The route engineering took into account the descent of the Serra do Mar aligned with the Sustainable Development Plan for the Paraná Coast, avoiding as much as possible the fragmentation of preservation areas. The choice of the most viable route, according to Fagundes, combined environmental impact modeling with traffic projections that point to 26 million tons on the main segment alone, a volume that rises to 38 million when short-distance internal traffic between rail yards is incorporated.

The short line concept, widespread in the United States and now being tested in Brazilian territory, is one of the operational pillars of Nova Ferroeste, with smaller branches connecting production hubs to the main trunks. The revitalization of the Cascavel and Guarapuava yards, where the connection with the Malha Sul occurs, is part of a modernization package that includes digital signaling, bridge reinforcement, and curvature adjustments to allow for longer and heavier trains.

International experience shows that this long-term concession model, combined with initial public investment and private participation in operation, has yielded consistent results in grain corridors. In the United States, states like Pennsylvania and North Carolina have structured specific grant programs for regional railroads, while Union Pacific advances in mergers to create more efficient transcontinental networks, as highlighted by Jim Vena, the company’s CEO, when arguing that innovative railroads are essential to sustain American economic growth.

The substantial difference in the Brazilian case is that the railway network is still nascent in terms of coverage density, making greenfield projects like Nova Ferroeste more transformative than simply modernizing a saturated network. While Europe and the United States concentrate investments in electrification, asset replacement, and ETCS signaling to extract 10% or 15% more capacity from existing corridors, Paraná is building from scratch a transportation backbone that could alter the logistics matrix of three states.

The B3 auction, scheduled for the second half of the year, will define the consortium that will take over the operation for nearly a century, with the obligation to complete the works and operate the corridor under contractually fixed performance and sustainability standards. The financial modeling, which allows the issuance of green bonds, signals a harmony between heavy engineering and capital markets that few infrastructure projects in Brazil have managed to achieve so far.

Nova Ferroeste is not just a freight railway; it is a laboratory of how Brazil can finance, build, and operate rail transportation infrastructure at scale, efficiency, and environmental responsibility. The country that historically abandoned its branch lines in favor of asphalt is beginning, with this project, to return to rails the role of silent protagonists of development.
